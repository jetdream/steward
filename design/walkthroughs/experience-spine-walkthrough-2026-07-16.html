03 · Your eyes here

Eight judgment calls

Each was decided with reasoning, not defaulted — but they're product-taste calls where your instinct may beat mine. The chosen option is marked; reply with a JC number to override or refine. Silence = the chosen option stands.

JC-1Approve is undoable until it publishes UI-29 · UI-42 — from the challenger's sharpest finding: Approve was the only irreversible verb in the product

A founder who spots a typo ten minutes after approving had exactly one tool before this fix: the kill switch — a panic lever for a routine mistake. Redirects had Undo, skips were recoverable, vetoes existed.

ChosenUndo in place until publish + recall from CalendarSymmetric with every other verb; “you can change anything until it goes out” becomes literally true. Cost: scheduled posts are mutable state until send.
AlternativeShort undo toast only (~5 min)Simpler mental model and implementation; but re-introduces a window where the only recourse is panic.

Speak up if you want approval to feel more final — some products argue commitment reduces second-guessing.

JC-2Where the learning loop shows its face UI-29 · APR-3 — “you've shortened 4 X posts in a row — make it a rule?”

The rule proposal is the moment the founder sees the system learning — arguably the product's best retention moment. It needs a home.

ChosenA digest card, in the stackArrives where attention already is, on cadence, with accept / edit / decline. Declined = not re-asked soon.
AlternativeChat-onlyMore conversational, but chat is opened irregularly — proposals would rot unseen.
AlternativeInline, immediately after the Nth editMaximum context, but interrupts the founder mid-task — the one time they're moving fast.

Speak up if you'd rather it interrupt at the moment of the pattern — immediacy versus flow protection.

JC-3Provenance on every card — density risk accepted UI-7 · UI-8 · DS-5 ProvenanceLine — your “yes” to provenance, extended to all drafts

You approved provenance; the open nuance was where. I put it on every card: the card already carries photo, text, ReasonLine, FitBadges, actions — one more quiet metadata line is real density.

ChosenEvery draft, alwaysUniformity is itself trust — a card without provenance would read as “this one it made up.” Kept to one muted line.
AlternativeOnly inferred & external contentLighter cards; provenance appears exactly where doubt is likely. But absence becomes a signal founders must learn.

Speak up if round-3 mockups feel cluttered — this is the first line I'd demote to tap-to-reveal.

JC-4Veto cards don't count and can't be batch-approved UI-29 · UI-7 — the TL1 card class you asked to design now

At TL1, auto-published items appear in the digest. I excluded them from the “3 of 5 reviewed” count and from “Approve all ready” — they need no action, and a batch verb that touches already-published things is a footgun.

ChosenInformational class: visible, excluded from count & batchThe count stays a promise (“what still needs you”); veto stays a deliberate, single-item act.
AlternativeCounted, with “acknowledge” as their verbForces awareness of what published autonomously — but converts earned autonomy back into homework, against G-3.

Speak up if you think unacknowledged auto-publishes are a bigger risk than the added friction.

JC-5Skip asks “why?” after the skip, never before UI-29 · DS-5 OptionalReason — your “yes” to skip reasons, with a timing decision

The reason chips appear after the skip completes — the card is already gone from the pending count; answering is a gift, not a toll. Same affordance reused for veto reasons (sibling coherence).

ChosenPost-action, dismissible, one-tapZero added friction to the verb; some reasons lost, all skips fast.
AlternativeReason as part of the skip gestureEvery skip teaches — but now skipping costs a decision, and G-3 pays for it.

Speak up if learning speed matters more than verb speed in the first cohort.

JC-6Settings lives inside the trust journey — no “manage settings” journey exists UI-12 homed under UI-38 · APR-2's selection is an affordance, not a flow

Autonomy is the gravitational center of Settings (trust levels, kill switch, pauses); cadence, notifications, channels, and billing share the surface as affordances. The alternative — a dedicated “manage” journey — would be a chapter nobody's life contains.

ChosenSettings = the trust journey's surfaceJourneys stay honest arcs; settings tasks are one-tap affordances documented in the screen body.
AlternativeA “configure Steward” journeyStructurally tidier coverage, experientially fictional — nobody journeys to a cadence dropdown.

Speak up if you expect settings to grow enough (multi-seat later?) to deserve its own arc.

JC-7After the one nudge: silence, and a graceful re-entry UI-32 · UI-57 — beyond APR-6's single-nudge rule

APR-6 mandates one nudge. I decided what happens after: a second missed week escalates nothing — the system quietly holds time-insensitive items, says so, and greets a returning founder with “here's what happened while you were away,” streak restarted without ceremony.

ChosenHold quietly, welcome back warmlyGuilt is this persona's churn engine; the product must be the one place that never generates it (VAL-5).
AlternativeOperator-mediated check-in (human email at week 3+)Cohort-1 retention lever; humans can say what software can't. Could coexist — an ops-console follow-up, not a product behavior.

Speak up if you want the operator check-in wired into UI-20 for cohort 1 — I'd support that as an ops practice.

JC-8Email is a first-class design element — “touchpoint” is now a pattern kind pattern.yaml · UI-54..57 — the outer loop, governed

The digest email is the most-seen artifact of the product, so I extended the project pattern with a touchpoint kind (nests in flows, like screens) rather than modeling emails as “screens” or leaving them as prose. Four exist: digest, photo request, veto notice, nudge.

ChosenDistinct kind, same governanceSemantic honesty (out-of-app ≠ screen); GR-7 conformance and the design gate apply to emails too.
AlternativeEmails as screensOne fewer kind; but “screen” starts lying, and email-specific constraints (clients, dark-mode mangling) get no home.

Speak up if the naming feels off — touchpoint vs notice vs message — it's cheap to rename now, expensive after specs cite it.
